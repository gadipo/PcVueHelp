Overview of 3D Modeling

3D modeling is a huge, rapidly developing and diversifying field of activity. It is employed in many business and recreational pursuits. Any experience you may have in 3D computer applications, or of navigating a craft or tool within a 3D space, will assist you with the principles and techniques that apply here.

A basic vocabulary is common across applications of 3D. So are many software functions, tools, utilities and programming interfaces, plus hardware components such as video (graphic) and multimedia PC cards. However, there is a gradation from general capabilities up to those requiring extreme performance plus specialized toolsets and the latest expertise. The 3D facilities in the HMI are situated toward the basic end of that scale.

You can insert 3D objects into the HMI and configure them, but the HMI does not have a capability for creating 3D objects.

Kinds of '3D' object

The 3D objects employed here consist of software files that are used to generate and display images on a 2D screen. These images can look solid, fluid or vaporous in whole or in part. This has nothing directly to do with objects having 3D appearances as follows:

  • 2D buttons, backgrounds and HMI drawing elements of PcVue that look as if they were sculpted and lighted. The '3D' symbols that can be displayed in the HMI's 2D mimics are like that.
  • Stereograms, which are usually double images viewed together to give an illusion of depth.
  • Holograms, which are images recorded and presented in three dimensions.

Limits of this Help book

This Help book is limited to the basic understanding needed to configure 3D models and mimics for industrial monitoring and control through the HMI.

  • It does not provide a full description of 3D technology with DirectX.
  • The information it does provide is often simplified for ease of reading.

It is structured and expressed as far as possible to resemble the Help for PcVue's 2D drawing, animation and tools. However, the explanations for things in the 3D world often have to cover features that have no equivalent in the 2D world. For those, a distinct set of concepts is necessary, with terminology to suit.

If you are starting from scratch, or from an understanding of 2D techniques, please refer to the Getting Started Manual for 3D Mimics.
