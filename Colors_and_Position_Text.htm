Colors and Positioning of 3D Text

3D text has some special considerations due to ways in which its behavior and implementation differ from those of other 3D drawing elements.

Persistence of color settings

Unless you chose different ones, the color you last applied to a text or line in a 2D or 3D drawing element is used for both Diffuse and Specular colors when you insert the next 3D text. The Emissive color is set to black unless you select another color for it.

Making the text visible

Like any other element in the HMI, a 3D text can only be seen and read easily if its font, colors and behavior are chosen appropriately.

Only TrueType and OpenType fonts can be used. They are marked with icons of and in the font list.

Where text is configured for billboarding, the font can be chosen on a similar basis to 2D text; otherwise, the depth of extrusion may favor a slim font over others.

In a particular project, the colors may have to conform to a color coding scheme or may be optimized for the application. In the latter case, grayscale colors, tones of a single dark color or sometimes contrasting colors can work well for the Diffuse and Specular colors, all with an Emissive color of black or near black. Where animations are applied that affect the colors etc. of a 3D element, those also have to be taken into account in the design.

Keeping the text visible

The background has to be taken into account in all cases. If a text is placed on or close to a larger surface, without billboarding, it may be simple to ensure a contrast of colors as required for 2D text. Any animation properties such as visibility and rotation also have to be taken into account.

Where a 3D text is not adjacent to any other elements, moves in any way or has billboarding turned on, you have to consider all backgrounds against which it may be presented from any viewing angle. With billboarding and multiple cameras, you may need to consider the 3D text's appearance in views from other cameras than the main one.

Deep extrusion makes text illegible from an angle, yet where billboarding is unsuitable, shallow extrusion makes the position of text visible from the sides.

A technique to cope with occlusion (hiding) is to apply partly transparent materials to the main elements, but keep the 3D texts opaque for legibility. For instance, a semi-transparent model of a building, installation or item of equipment may be located on contrasting opaque ground and its components may be labeled with opaque 3D texts.
