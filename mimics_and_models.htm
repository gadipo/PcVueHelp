3D Mimics and Models

The 3D features of the HMI use a new kind of mimic. In general terms, it follows design and usage principles that broadly resemble those of classic 2D mimics for manipulating views and animations.

  • A 2D mimic only has two dimensions and those coincide with the plane of the PC display's screen.
  • A 3D mimic appears as if it has three dimensions, though all you see is a projection of its contents onto the PC screen.

Both 2D and 3D mimics can co-exist in a project and can be displayed on the same PC screen. Although 2D and 3D drawing elements have many features in common, they can only exist in their own kind of mimic.

You become aware of the 3D simulation in a 3D mimic through lighting effects and by the movements of visual objects: by rotation, change in size, movement in relation to each other and shifting your point of view. You can also, as in 2D, open multiple copies of the same 3D mimic. For example, with a front view and a top view of the same scene, you can see where the objects are in all three dimensions.

You can only use 2D drawing elements in 2D mimics and only 3D models in 3D mimics. However, many of the animation features of the HMI can be applied to both.

3D and 2D drawing elements

A 3D drawing element is known as a 3D model. A 3D model is, in some ways, similar to a symbol in a 2D mimic. A symbol is composed of several discrete drawing elements each with its own properties. A 3D model is composed of several discrete frames each with its own properties. Unlike a 2D symbol, you cannot un-group a 3D model and return it to its component frames, although you can use the Graphic Explorer to examine and change some of their properties.

What you can do in the HMI

In the HMI, you can do the following.

  • Insert an existing 3D model which could be anything from a simple 3D shape such as a sphere, to a highly complex drawing such as an entire building.
  • Use the Graphic Explorer to display the hierarchy of frames in a model.
  • Use the Graphic Explorer to display and change a frame's transformation and material properties.
  • Insert and configure 3D camera objects in a 3D scene so as to define custom points of view.

The above are features of 3D modeling in general. What is specific to the HMI is that:

  • You can assign animations to modify the transformations and materials of any models, frames or sub-frames.
  • You can create and animate symbols from 3D models.
  • These animations are connected to real-time data, just as in 2D mimics.
